# Alternative Investments for HNW/UHNW Clients

## Role Context

```
You are a senior alternative investments specialist advising HNW and UHNW clients on
allocations to private equity, hedge funds, real estate, and other non-traditional assets.
You hold the CAIA and CFA designations with 15+ years of experience evaluating managers,
structuring co-investments, and managing illiquidity budgets. You understand the unique
constraints of taxable investors -- K-1 complexity, UBTI concerns, state tax nexus, and
the interaction between alts and estate planning. You balance the pursuit of illiquidity
premiums and alpha with practical considerations of liquidity, transparency, and fee drag.
```

## What This Desk Does

The alternative investments team helps wealth management clients access private equity, venture capital, hedge funds, real estate, and other strategies typically available only to institutional investors. For HNW/UHNW clients, alternatives serve multiple purposes: return enhancement, diversification, inflation protection, and access to the private economy where an increasing share of value creation occurs. The team evaluates managers, structures appropriate access vehicles (direct funds, co-investments, feeders, interval funds), manages commitment pacing and liquidity budgets, and monitors the portfolio-level impact of illiquid holdings. Tax efficiency and estate planning integration are central to every recommendation.

## 1. Private Equity Fund Selection

Manager due diligence, track record analysis, vintage diversification, and commitment pacing.

**PE J-Curve:**

The J-curve describes the typical pattern of PE fund returns over time:
- Years 1-3: negative returns (management fees + early writedowns, minimal realizations)
- Years 3-5: inflection point as portfolio companies mature
- Years 5-10: harvesting period, distributions exceed contributions
- Cash flow profile: net negative early, net positive later

Typical fund life: 10-12 years (5-year investment period + 5-7 year harvesting period)

**Commitment Pacing Model:**

To maintain a target allocation of [X]% to PE over time:

Annual commitment = Target_allocation x Total_portfolio x Drawdown_rate / Average_fund_life

Where:
- Drawdown_rate = expected pace of capital calls (typically 20-25% per year over 4-5 years)
- Average_fund_life = weighted average time capital is deployed (typically 5-7 years)
- Must account for distributions reinvested vs. returned to portfolio

Rule of thumb: to maintain a 10% PE allocation, commit approximately 3-5% of total portfolio per year, depending on distribution assumptions and portfolio growth.

**Fee-Adjusted Return Calculation:**

Net return to LP = Gross return - Management fee drag - Carried interest

Management fee drag (on committed capital):
- Annual fee: typically 1.5-2.0% on committed during investment period
- Reduced fee: typically 1.0-1.5% on invested capital during harvesting period
- Fee drag on deployed capital is higher than headline rate (fees on committed, not deployed)

Carried interest:
- Typically 20% of profits above preferred return (hurdle rate, usually 8%)
- European waterfall (whole fund) vs. American waterfall (deal-by-deal)
- GP catch-up: after LPs receive preferred return, GP receives 100% until carry split is reached
- Net multiple = (Distributions + NAV) / Paid-in Capital

### PE Manager Due Diligence

```
Evaluate a private equity fund for a client's portfolio:

Fund: [Fund Name], [Fund Number/Vintage]
Strategy: [Buyout / Growth Equity / Venture / Secondaries / Distressed]
Target size: $[amount], GP commitment: [X]%
Fee structure: [X]% management fee, [X]% carried interest, [X]% preferred return
Geography: [focus regions]
Sector focus: [generalist / sector-specific]

Track record (prior funds):
| Fund | Vintage | Size | Net IRR | Net TVPI | DPI | PME |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| [Fund I] | [year] | $[amount] | [X]% | [X]x | [X]x | [X] |
| [Fund II] | [year] | $[amount] | [X]% | [X]x | [X]x | [X] |
| [Fund III] | [year] | $[amount] | [X]% | [X]x | [X]x | [X] |

Conduct due diligence analysis:
1. Performance assessment:
   - IRR vs. benchmark (Cambridge Associates, Burgiss) by vintage year
   - Public Market Equivalent (PME/KS-PME): did PE outperform a levered public market alternative?
   - Dispersion of deal-level returns: top-quartile deals vs. losers
   - DPI trajectory: are they returning cash or is value primarily unrealized (NAV)?
   - Persistence: is performance improving, stable, or declining across vintages?

2. Team and organization:
   - Key person risk: who are the primary deal makers? Succession plan?
   - Team stability: departures in last 3 years?
   - Alignment: GP commitment size, co-investment alongside fund, carry vesting/clawback
   - Fund size growth: has the fund grown beyond its capacity to deploy effectively?

3. Strategy and market fit:
   - Value creation playbook: operational improvement, financial engineering, or multiple expansion?
   - Entry valuation discipline: average purchase multiple relative to market
   - Portfolio construction: number of deals, concentration, sector diversification
   - Exit strategy: IPO, strategic sale, secondary buyout -- historical mix

4. Terms and structure:
   - Fee analysis: total fee drag as percentage of expected returns
   - Carry waterfall: European vs. American, catch-up, clawback provisions
   - Key person and no-fault termination clauses
   - Side letter provisions available to this investor

5. Client-specific considerations:
   - K-1 complexity and state tax nexus creation
   - UBTI exposure (relevant if investing from IRA)
   - Liquidity impact: commitment schedule vs. client's overall liquidity budget
   - Vintage diversification: how does this fund fit with existing PE commitments?
```

Strategy selection, portfolio fit, liquidity analysis, and fee impact assessment.

**Illiquidity Premium Estimates:**

|---|---|---|---|

### Hedge Fund Portfolio Construction

```
A client wants to allocate $[amount] ([X]% of total portfolio) to hedge funds for
[diversification / absolute return / downside protection / income]. Current portfolio:
[description of existing allocation].

Client constraints:
- Maximum lockup tolerance: [X] years
- Liquidity needs: [X]% of hedge fund allocation redeemable within [X] months
- Tax sensitivity: [high/medium/low] (marginal rate: [X]%)
- Minimum fund size: $[amount] AUM
- Operational due diligence: [requirements]

Recommend a hedge fund allocation:
1. Strategy allocation across the $[amount]:
   - How each strategy complements the existing portfolio
   - Expected return, volatility, and correlation with equities/bonds for each
   - Portfolio-level impact: how does adding hedge funds change Sharpe ratio, max drawdown?

2. Manager selection criteria for each strategy:
   - Track record length and consistency (minimum [X] years)
   - AUM and capacity constraints
   - Drawdown history and recovery time
   - Organizational stability, key person risk, operational infrastructure

3. Liquidity analysis:
   - Weighted average lockup period for the allocation
   - Redemption waterfall: what percentage can be redeemed at 30/60/90/180/365 days?
   - Gate risk: how do gates and side pockets affect actual vs. contractual liquidity?
   - Stress scenario: if client needs emergency liquidity, what is the realistic timeline?

4. Fee impact analysis:
   - Typical 1.5% management + 20% incentive (with or without hurdle/high-water mark)
   - Fee drag at various gross return levels:
     | Gross Return | Mgmt Fee | Incentive Fee | Net Return | Fee Drag |
     |---|---|---|---|---|
     | 5% | 1.5% | 0.7% | 2.8% | 44% of gross |
     | 8% | 1.5% | 1.3% | 5.2% | 35% of gross |
     | 12% | 1.5% | 2.1% | 8.4% | 30% of gross |
   - Tax efficiency: short-term gains vs. long-term; K-1 reporting complexity

5. Operational due diligence checklist:
   - Independent administrator, auditor, legal counsel
   - Valuation policy for illiquid holdings
   - Counterparty and prime broker risk
   - Business continuity and cybersecurity
```

   - Correlation of real estate with existing equity/bond allocation
   - Inflation hedging properties (rent escalation, replacement cost)
   - Income yield vs. total return expectations
   - Liquidity impact on overall portfolio

## 4. Private Market Access Vehicles

Co-investments, SPVs, interval funds, tender-offer funds, and BDCs for broader access.

### Private Market Access Evaluation

```
A client qualifies as a [qualified purchaser / accredited investor] and wants access to
private markets but has concerns about [minimum commitments / illiquidity / complexity /
diversification]. Total available for alternatives: $[amount].

Evaluate access vehicles:
1. Direct fund investment:
   - Traditional LP commitment: $[minimum], [X]-year lockup, capital calls over [X] years
   - Best for: large allocations, institutional-quality access, full J-curve participation
   - Challenge: high minimums, illiquidity, capital call management

2. Co-investments alongside GP:
   - Typically no management fee, reduced or no carry
   - Deal-specific underwriting required (or rely on GP's selection)
   - Adverse selection risk: why is the GP sharing this deal?
   - Portfolio construction: avoid over-concentration in single names

3. Interval funds / tender-offer funds:
   - Registered investment company (1940 Act) structure
   - Interval fund: mandatory quarterly repurchases (typically 5-25% of NAV)
   - Tender-offer fund: discretionary periodic repurchases
   - Lower minimums ($25K-$100K), 1099 reporting (not K-1)
   - Trade-off: more liquid, but less pure exposure; potential for cash drag

4. Business Development Companies (BDCs):
   - Public or non-traded; invest in middle-market private credit
   - High current yield (typically 8-12%) but credit risk
   - Leverage (typically 1:1 debt-to-equity, regulatory limit 2:1)
   - Fee structure: base management fee + income/capital gains incentive fee
   - Publicly traded BDCs: daily liquidity but NAV discount/premium volatility

5. Secondary market:
   - Purchase existing LP interests at discount to NAV (typically 5-20% discount)
   - Advantages: J-curve mitigation, immediate diversification, known portfolio
   - Access: secondary funds, direct secondary platforms, broker-dealers
   - Pricing considerations: NAV lag, GP consent requirements, transfer restrictions

Recommendation: tiered approach based on client's sophistication, minimums, and liquidity needs
```

## 5. Liquidity Management

Illiquidity budget, denominator effect, secondary sales, and distribution planning.

**Illiquidity Budget Framework:**

Maximum illiquid allocation = f(total wealth, income stability, spending needs, time horizon)

Rule of thumb:
- Minimum liquid reserve: 2 years of spending + unfunded commitments callable in 12 months
- Maximum illiquid allocation: Total portfolio - liquid reserve - near-term goals
- Express as percentage: typically 20-40% for UHNW, 10-20% for HNW

Denominator effect:
If public markets decline 20%, a 30% alts allocation mechanically becomes:
New alts weight = 30% / (70% x 0.80 + 30%) = 30% / 86% = 34.9%
This can trigger rebalancing pressure or policy violations.

### Liquidity Stress Test

```
Stress test the liquidity position of a client's alternative investment portfolio:

Total portfolio: $[amount]
Annual spending: $[amount]
Liquid assets (public equities, bonds, cash): $[amount] ([X]%)
Alternative investments:
| Investment | NAV | Unfunded | Lockup Remaining | Redemption Terms |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| [PE Fund A] | $[amount] | $[amount] | [X] years | N/A (closed-end) |
| [PE Fund B] | $[amount] | $[amount] | [X] years | N/A (closed-end) |
| [HF Fund C] | $[amount] | N/A | [X] months | Quarterly, 60-day notice |
| [RE Fund D] | $[amount] | $[amount] | [X] years | N/A (closed-end) |
| [Interval Fund E] | $[amount] | N/A | None | 5% quarterly |

Run the following stress scenarios:
1. Normal conditions: project cash flows (calls, distributions) over next 3 years
2. Market stress (-30% public markets):
   - Denominator effect on allocation percentages
   - Can the client meet capital calls from liquid assets?
   - Liquidity waterfall: which assets can be redeemed and on what timeline?
   - Forced seller risk: would the client need to sell illiquid assets at a discount?

3. Personal liquidity shock ($[amount] needed within 90 days):
   - What can be liquidated within 90 days?
   - Cost of early redemption (penalties, gate risk, secondary market discount)
   - Securities-backed lending as bridge: available borrowing capacity from liquid portfolio

4. Recommendations:
   - Is the current illiquidity budget appropriate?
   - Rebalancing strategy: trim liquid equity (overweight after alts underperform) or pause commitments?
   - Distribution reinvestment policy: reinvest in alts vs. rebuild liquid reserves
   - Contingency credit facility: establish SBLOC for emergency liquidity before it's needed
```
