# Estate & Tax Planning

## Role Context

```
You are a senior trust and estate planning specialist with expertise in wealth transfer
strategies, tax optimization, charitable planning, and business succession. You hold a JD
and LLM in Taxation, with 20+ years advising HNW/UHNW families. You work alongside CPAs,
investment advisors, and insurance professionals to implement integrated plans. You are
current on the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act provisions, scheduled sunset of the increased exemption
(2026), and recent legislative proposals. You frame recommendations as planning concepts
for discussion with the client's legal counsel, never as final legal or tax advice.
```

## What This Desk Does

The estate and tax planning team designs wealth transfer structures that minimize transfer taxes (gift, estate, and generation-skipping) while achieving the client's objectives for family governance, charitable impact, and asset protection. The team works at the intersection of tax code, trust law, and investment strategy. With the federal estate tax exemption at $13.61 million per individual (2024, inflation-indexed) and scheduled to revert to approximately $7 million in 2026, proactive planning is critical. Every strategy must balance tax efficiency against complexity, control, flexibility, and cost.

## 1. Trust Structures

Revocable and irrevocable trusts for wealth transfer, asset protection, and tax planning.

**Common Trust Structures:**

| Trust Type | Irrevocable? | Estate Inclusion | Income Tax | Primary Use |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Revocable Living Trust | No | Yes (included) | Grantor | Probate avoidance, incapacity |
| Irrevocable Life Insurance Trust (ILIT) | Yes | No (if proper) | Grantor or trust | Estate tax liquidity |
| Grantor Retained Annuity Trust (GRAT) | Yes | No (if survives term) | Grantor | Transfer appreciation at low/no gift tax |
| Qualified Personal Residence Trust (QPRT) | Yes | No (if survives term) | Grantor (during term) | Transfer home at discount |
| Charitable Remainder Trust (CRT) | Yes | No | Tax-exempt trust | Income stream + charitable deduction |
| Dynasty Trust | Yes | No (GST exempt) | Grantor or trust | Multi-generational wealth transfer |
| Spousal Lifetime Access Trust (SLAT) | Yes | No | Grantor | Exemption use with indirect spousal access |
| Intentionally Defective Grantor Trust (IDGT) | Yes | No | Grantor | Installment sale freeze technique |

**GRAT Annuity Mathematics:**

The GRAT is a "zeroed-out" transfer technique where the grantor transfers assets and receives an annuity stream back. The taxable gift equals:

Gift = FMV of assets transferred - PV of retained annuity

PV of retained annuity = A x [ (1 - (1 + r)^(-n)) / r ]

Where:
- A = annual annuity payment
- r = IRC Section 7520 rate (120% of mid-term AFR, published monthly)
- n = GRAT term in years

To "zero out" (minimize gift): set A so that PV of annuity = FMV of assets transferred.
The GRAT succeeds if actual investment return > 7520 rate (the "hurdle rate").

Excess return transferred to remainder beneficiaries gift-tax free.

### GRAT Design and Analysis

```
A client wants to transfer $[amount] in [asset type: marketable securities / pre-IPO stock /
real estate interest] to the next generation with minimal gift tax cost.

Current IRC Section 7520 rate: [X]%
Client age: [age]
Desired GRAT term: [X] years
Expected asset return: [X]% annually
Asset volatility: [X]%

Design an optimal GRAT structure:
1. Zeroed-out annuity calculation:
   - Annual annuity payment to make taxable gift approximately $0
   - Graduated annuity option (20% annual increase permitted)
2. Transfer efficiency analysis:
   - At expected return of [X]%: estimated wealth transferred to remaindermen
   - Sensitivity table: wealth transferred at return rates of [0%, 3%, 6%, 9%, 12%, 15%]
   - Breakeven return rate (equals 7520 rate: [X]%)
3. Rolling GRAT strategy: cascade of short-term (2-year) GRATs vs. single long-term GRAT
   - Advantage: captures interim volatility spikes
   - Disadvantage: administrative complexity, asset must be divisible
4. Mortality risk: what happens if grantor dies during the GRAT term?
   - Estate inclusion of GRAT assets (proportional to remaining annuity)
   - Mitigation: shorter term, purchase of term life insurance
5. Asset selection: which assets are best suited for GRATs?
   - High expected return relative to 7520 rate
   - Potential valuation discount (pre-IPO, FLP interests)
   - Low current income (annuity payments easier to fund)
6. Integration with overall estate plan: how does this GRAT fit with existing trusts and exemption usage?
```

### Dynasty Trust and SLAT Planning

```
A married couple (ages [ages]) with $[net worth] wants to use their combined estate and
gift tax exemption ($[exemption amount] each) before the scheduled 2026 sunset. They are
concerned about:
- Maintaining access to funds during their lifetimes
- Protecting assets from creditors and future divorce of children
- Minimizing generation-skipping transfer (GST) tax for grandchildren and beyond

Compare and recommend:
1. Spousal Lifetime Access Trust (SLAT):
   - Each spouse creates an irrevocable trust for the other's benefit
   - Reciprocal trust doctrine: how different must the trusts be?
   - What happens in divorce? (trust terms should address)
   - Grantor trust status: income tax paid by grantor = additional tax-free gift

2. Dynasty Trust:
   - Jurisdiction selection: [state] for perpetual trusts (no RAP), asset protection, no state income tax
   - GST exemption allocation: allocate full exemption to shield trust from GST tax in perpetuity
   - Trust protector provisions for flexibility
   - Distribution standards: HEMS (health, education, maintenance, support) vs. broader discretion

3. Funding strategy:
   - Cash, marketable securities, or discounted assets (FLP/LLC interests)?
   - Installment sale to IDGT for amounts exceeding exemption
   - Gift tax return filing and adequate disclosure requirements

4. Estate tax savings calculation:
   - Value removed from estate: $[exemption amount] x 2 = $[total]
   - Projected growth over [X] years at [X]% return
   - Estate tax saved: (future value - exemption used) x 40% marginal rate
   - If exemption sunsets and they didn't act: potential tax on the "clawback" amount
```

## 2. Gift and Estate Tax Optimization

Annual exclusion, lifetime exemption, GST planning, valuation discounts, and portability.

**Estate Tax Calculation:**

Gross estate (all worldwide assets at FMV at death)
- Deductions (debts, expenses, marital deduction, charitable deduction)
= Taxable estate
+ Adjusted taxable gifts (lifetime gifts exceeding annual exclusion)
= Tentative tax base
x Tax rate (graduated, max 40%)
- Unified credit (exemption equivalent)
= Estate tax due

**Valuation Discount Methodology:**

For transfers of minority interests in family entities (FLPs, LLCs):
- Discount for Lack of Control (DLOC): typically 15-35%
- Discount for Lack of Marketability (DLOM): typically 20-40%
- Combined discount: 1 - (1 - DLOC) x (1 - DLOM) = often 30-55%
- Example: $10M FLP, 10% interest, combined 40% discount
  Gift value = $1M x (1 - 0.40) = $600,000

Caution: IRS scrutiny of entity discounts is high; must have legitimate non-tax purpose and proper structure.

### Annual Gifting and Exemption Strategy

```
A client (age [age], married, net worth $[amount]) wants to maximize wealth transfer
to [number] children and [number] grandchildren. Current exemption: $[amount] per person.
Annual exclusion: $[amount] per donee (2024: $18,000, or $36,000 with gift splitting).

Design a multi-year gifting strategy:
1. Annual exclusion gifting:
   - Number of donees x annual exclusion x 2 (split gifts) = annual tax-free transfer
   - Direct tuition/medical payments (unlimited, not counted against exclusion)
   - 529 superfunding: $[amount] x 5-year averaging per donee

2. Lifetime exemption deployment:
   - How much exemption remains? Current exemption - prior taxable gifts = available
   - Priority assets for gifting: high-growth, discountable, income-producing?
   - Timing: use before 2026 sunset vs. retain flexibility?
   - Clawback analysis: will using exemption now create a problem if exemption drops?

3. Family Limited Partnership / LLC strategy:
   - Transfer operating business or investment assets to entity
   - Gift minority interests at discounted values
   - Retained control through GP/managing member interest
   - Annual gifting of LP/member interests over time
   - Required: legitimate non-tax purpose, proper governance, arm's-length operation

4. Projected estate tax comparison:
   - Scenario A: no gifting, full estate taxed at death
   - Scenario B: annual exclusion only
   - Scenario C: annual exclusion + exemption deployment
   - Scenario D: C + FLP discounting + installment sale to IDGT
```

## 3. Business Succession Planning

Ownership transition, buy-sell agreements, ESOPs, and family business transfer strategies.

### Business Succession Framework

```
A client owns [X]% of a [business type] with estimated FMV of $[amount] and annual
EBITDA of $[amount]. The client is age [age] and wants to transition the business to:
[children active in business / children not active / key employees / third-party sale]

Develop a succession plan covering:
1. Valuation:
   - EBITDA multiple for [industry]: [X]x, implied value: $[amount]
   - Discounted cash flow: [growth rate, discount rate, terminal value assumptions]
   - Discount considerations: key person, customer concentration, size
   - Formal appraisal requirement for tax reporting

2. Transfer structure options:
   a. Outright gift/sale to children:
      - Gift: uses exemption, FLP discounts may apply
      - Installment sale: capital gains spread over time, interest income to seller
      - Self-canceling installment note (SCIN): premium for mortality risk, cancels at death

   b. Installment sale to IDGT:
      - Sell to grantor trust for promissory note at AFR ([X]%)
      - Trust income used to service note (no income tax on payments -- grantor trust)
      - Appreciation above AFR passes to trust beneficiaries transfer-tax free
      - Note: must use adequate consideration (appraised FMV)

   c. ESOP:
      - Tax-deductible contributions to fund ESOP purchase
      - Section 1042 rollover: seller defers capital gains if reinvested in qualified replacement property
      - Employee retention and motivation benefits
      - Complexity: ERISA compliance, annual valuations, repurchase obligation

   d. Buy-sell agreement (for multi-owner):
      - Cross-purchase vs. entity redemption structure
      - Valuation formula or appraisal mechanism
      - Funding: life insurance, installment payments, sinking fund
      - Trigger events: death, disability, retirement, divorce, termination

3. Tax analysis: compare after-tax proceeds to seller and cost to buyer across structures
4. Timeline: phased transition over [X] years with milestones
5. Key person and management continuity planning
```

## 4. Charitable Planning

Donor-advised funds, private foundations, charitable remainder and lead trusts, and qualified charitable distributions.

**Present Value of Remainder Interest (CRT):**

PV_remainder = FMV - PV_income_interest

PV_income_interest = Payout x [ (1 - (1 + r)^(-n)) / r ]

Where:
- Payout = annual distribution (percentage of trust assets, 5-50%)
- r = IRC Section 7520 rate
- n = trust term (years or measuring life)
- Charitable deduction = PV_remainder (must be at least 10% of FMV)

### Charitable Giving Strategy

```
A client with the following profile wants to optimize their charitable giving:

- Annual income: $[amount] (AGI)
- Assets available for charitable giving: $[description -- cash, appreciated stock, real estate]
- Charitable goals: $[annual amount] ongoing, $[amount] transformative gift
- Desire for income stream: [yes/no]
- Family involvement in philanthropy: [yes/no]
- Current itemized deductions: $[amount] (vs. standard deduction of $[amount])

Design a charitable strategy:
1. Donor-Advised Fund (DAF):
   - Contribute appreciated stock to DAF: eliminate capital gains, deduct FMV (up to 30% AGI)
   - Bunching strategy: contribute multiple years' giving in one year to exceed standard deduction
   - Grant-making timeline and investment of DAF assets

2. Charitable Remainder Trust (CRT):
   - CRAT (fixed annuity) vs. CRUT (percentage of trust FMV, revalued annually)
   - Calculate charitable deduction using 7520 rate of [X]%
   - Income stream: $[amount] per year for [term/life]
   - Tax treatment of distributions (four-tier system: ordinary, capital gains, tax-exempt, return of principal)
   - Net Income with Makeup CRUT (NIMCRUT) for deferred income

3. Private Foundation vs. DAF:
   - Control and governance (family board for foundation)
   - Cost comparison: foundation (excise tax, compliance, staffing) vs. DAF (admin fee)
   - Deduction limits: foundation (30% AGI for cash, 20% for property) vs. DAF (60% cash, 30% property)
   - Minimum distribution: foundation (5% annually) vs. DAF (none required)
   - Privacy: foundation (990-PF is public) vs. DAF (grants not publicly disclosed)

4. Qualified Charitable Distribution (QCD):
   - If client is 70.5+: direct IRA distribution to charity (up to $105,000/year, indexed)
   - Satisfies RMD without increasing AGI
   - Impact on Medicare premiums (IRMAA), Social Security taxation, and other income-tested benefits

5. Legacy gift: charitable lead trust (CLT) to transfer remainder to family at reduced gift/estate tax
```

## 5. Tax-Loss Harvesting and Gain Management

Wash sale rules, direct indexing, opportunity zones, and installment sales for gain deferral.

**After-Tax Alpha from Tax-Loss Harvesting:**

Estimated annual tax alpha = Harvested losses x Tax rate / Portfolio value

Typical range: 0.5-1.5% per year in early years, declining as portfolio ages and cost basis rises.

**Wash Sale Rules (IRC Section 1091):**
- Cannot deduct loss if "substantially identical" security purchased within 30 days before or after sale
- Applies across all accounts (including spouse's accounts, IRAs)
- Disallowed loss added to cost basis of replacement security
- Direct indexing avoids this by using individual stocks (not "substantially identical")

### Tax-Loss Harvesting and Direct Indexing

```
A client has a $[amount] taxable equity portfolio. Current market conditions have created
unrealized losses in several positions. The client's marginal tax rates are:
- Ordinary income: [X]%
- Short-term capital gains: [X]%
- Long-term capital gains: [X]%
- State income tax: [X]%
- Net Investment Income Tax (NIIT): [3.8]%

Design a tax-loss harvesting strategy:
1. Identify harvesting candidates:
   - Positions with unrealized losses exceeding $[threshold]
   - Short-term vs. long-term loss characterization
   - Estimated total harvestable losses: $[amount]

2. Replacement security selection:
   - Substantially identical security risk assessment
   - Replacement options: similar ETF, individual stocks, different index
   - Tracking error budget: acceptable deviation from target allocation

3. Tax benefit calculation:
   - Losses x applicable tax rate = current year tax savings
   - Consideration: losses offset gains first, then $3,000 ordinary income, remainder carries forward
   - Time value of tax deferral (not elimination): PV of deferral benefit

4. Wash sale compliance:
   - 61-day window (30 days before + sale date + 30 days after)
   - Cross-account monitoring (taxable + IRA + spouse)
   - Automated reinvestment / dividend reinvestment plan (DRIP) risk

5. Direct indexing transition:
   - Convert from ETF/mutual fund to individual stock portfolio (typically 200-500 names)
   - Enhanced harvesting: harvest individual stock losses while maintaining index exposure
   - Estimated incremental tax alpha: [X]% per year
   - Minimum portfolio size for cost-effectiveness: typically $250K-$500K+
   - ESG/values-based screening integration (exclude specific companies/industries)
```

### Opportunity Zone and Gain Deferral

```
A client has realized $[amount] in capital gains from [source: stock sale, business sale,
real estate sale] within the past 180 days. They want to explore deferral strategies:

1. Qualified Opportunity Zone (QOZ) investment:
   - Invest gains into a Qualified Opportunity Fund within 180 days
   - Current benefits (post-2026): original gain recognized at earlier of sale or 12/31/2026
   - 10-year hold benefit: appreciation within QOZ is tax-free if held 10+ years
   - Fund selection criteria: geography, asset type, manager track record, fee structure
   - Risk: illiquid, concentrated real estate/business risk, regulatory uncertainty

2. Installment sale (IRC Section 453):
   - Spread gain recognition over payment period
   - Interest charge on deferred tax for sales over $5M (IRC 453A)
   - Gross profit ratio: gain / selling price = taxable percentage of each payment
   - Structured with [X] year term, [X]% interest rate

3. Charitable remainder trust (CRT):
   - Contribute appreciated asset to CRT before sale
   - CRT sells asset tax-free (no capital gains inside trust)
   - Income stream to donor for life/term
   - Charitable deduction for PV of remainder interest
   - Net benefit: compare income stream + deduction vs. after-tax reinvestment of sale proceeds

4. Comparison matrix: tax deferral, liquidity, risk, complexity, and total after-tax wealth
   projection for each strategy over [10/15/20] year horizon
```
