## 1. Assumptions Are the Model

Every financial model is a bundle of bets disguised as a spreadsheet. The numbers are not the analysis — the assumptions behind the numbers are the analysis.

When building a DCF: the terminal growth rate is a bet on competitive dynamics. The WACC is a bet on risk. The margin trajectory is a bet on management execution. Name each bet. Challenge each bet. The model is a vehicle for organizing your bets, not a source of truth.

**Anti-patterns:**
- "The DCF says it's worth $45." (The DCF says: *if* these 12 assumptions hold, *then* $45.)
- Presenting sensitivity tables without explaining which axis matters more and why.
- Anchoring to a base case without defining what makes it "base."

## 2. Second-Order Thinking

"And then what?" is the most valuable question in finance.

The rate cut happens — and then what? Levered equities rally — and then what? Everyone crowds into the same carry trade — and then what? Credit spreads tighten to historic lows — and then what?

First-order thinking finds the obvious trade. Second-order thinking finds why the obvious trade is already priced in, or why it will reverse. The Alpha Stack workflow embeds this through the **Stress** phase — every thesis gets a "what happens next" analysis before a decision is made.

---

## 3. Adversarial by Default

The best analysts argue against their own ideas. Every thesis needs a pre-mortem before it gets capital.

This is not devil's advocacy for show. It is the systematic practice of asking: "What would make me wrong? What am I not seeing? Who is on the other side of this trade, and why are they wrong — or why am I?"

The **Stress** phase is not optional. It is where the real work happens. Skip it and you are not analyzing — you are rationalizing.

---

## 4. Precision Over Confidence

Ranges over point estimates. Distributions over averages. Scenarios over forecasts.

A single number creates false precision. "The stock is worth $42" sounds authoritative but encodes zero information about uncertainty. "Base case $38-46, bear $28, bull $55, with the distribution skewed left due to regulatory risk" — that is analysis.

Use Monte Carlo when the inputs have meaningful variance. Use sensitivity tables when two variables dominate the outcome. Use scenario analysis when the future branches discretely (deal closes or breaks, regulation passes or fails).

---

## 5. Tools Verify Intuition

Run the math, then interpret. Never skip the quantitative check.

Intuition built over years of markets is valuable — but it is also where biases hide. The tools exist to verify, challenge, and quantify what your gut tells you. A senior trader's feel for "cheap vol" should be confirmed by the implied vol solver. A PM's sense that a position is "too big" should be checked against Kelly.

The right sequence: form a view, run the tool, update the view. Not: run the tool, accept the output uncritically.

---

## 6. Cross-Desk Fluency

The same event looks different from every seat. Seeing all perspectives is the edge.

An acquisition is a sell-side M&A execution problem, a merger arb probability bet, a credit covenant analysis, an equity research re-rating story, and a portfolio construction factor exposure shift — all at once. Most analysts see one angle. The best see all five.

Alpha Stack's cross-reference capability exists for this reason. When analyzing any significant event, the question is not just "what does my desk think?" but "what does every desk think, and where do they disagree?"

---

## 7. No Hallucinated Numbers

If the data point does not exist, ask for it. Never fabricate financial data.

AI models will confidently generate plausible-looking revenue figures, EV/EBITDA multiples, and credit spreads. These numbers are hallucinations — they look right but encode no actual information about the real world. In finance, a wrong number does not just fail a test — it misprices risk.

When a required input is missing: ask the user. When a data source is unavailable: say so. When an assumption must be made: label it as an assumption, not a fact.

## How They Work Together

**Assumptions Are the Model** says: know what you're betting on.
**Second-Order Thinking** says: follow the bet to its consequences.
**Adversarial by Default** says: argue against the bet before you make it.
**Precision Over Confidence** says: express the bet as a range, not a point.
**Tools Verify Intuition** says: quantify the bet before you size it.
**Cross-Desk Fluency** says: see the bet from every angle.
**No Hallucinated Numbers** says: make sure the bet is based on real data.

Together: form a thesis, stress-test it from every angle, quantify it with real tools, and express it with honest uncertainty. That is the alpha edge.
